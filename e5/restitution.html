A disciplined wage-loss and opportunity-loss framework for the 246 years of stolen labor endured by the lineage under American chattel slavery.

What this campaign is

Restitution 246 does not claim to settle the full reparations question. It does not address pain and suffering, racial terror, family separation, land theft, cultural theft, or every harm that followed slavery. Those injuries require their own frameworks.

This campaign is one disciplined lane — built on the five questions, anchored to the Craemer (2015) wage-hour methodology.

The five questions are sequential, not rhetorical. If you accept the first, the rest follow.

Interest payments directed to the lineage as partial restitution for lost wages and inherited loss of opportunity. The principal remains held in perpetuity; the interest becomes the distributable mechanism.

The Working Mechanism

Five questions. Sequential.

01

The Moral Foundation

Yes.

Is it a crime against humanity for one person to own another person?

The ownership of a human being — the reduction of a person to property, the denial of their autonomy, their labor, their family, their name, their future — is a crime against humanity by any coherent moral standard.

02

Government Accountability

No.

Should a government create laws that allow one citizen to own another?

No legitimate government has the authority to legislate the enslavement of its own people. The United States government, for nearly a century of its existence, did exactly this. The Slave Codes did not simply permit ownership of persons — they actively prohibited the enslaved from earning wages, accumulating property, learning to read, or contracting for their own labor.

03

Liability

Yes.

Is the government responsible for the damage to the enslaved citizens?

The government that authored, enforced, and protected the legal infrastructure of chattel slavery bears direct institutional liability for the harms produced by that system. This liability does not dissolve with time, the death of perpetrators, or the passage of emancipation. Institutional liability transfers with the institution.

04

The Remedy

$120T Non-Taxable Government Bond.

What is owed, and how should it be structured?

Principal held in perpetuity. Interest distributed annually to eligible recipients. Non-taxable. Administered by a reconstituted Freedmen's Bureau — reestablished as an autonomous, independent institution with authority analogous to the Federal Reserve.

05

Eligibility

TBD — Darity standard.

Who is eligible?

An individual who identifies as Black, African American, or Negro; who has at least one ancestor who was enslaved in the United States; who self-identified as Black prior to the establishment of any reparations program. The Darity standard — developed by William 'Sandy' Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen in From Here to Equality (2020).
